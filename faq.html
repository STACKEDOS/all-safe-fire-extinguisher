Fire Extinguisher FAQ — California Businesses

Answers to the most common questions we hear from business owners across Southern California about fire extinguisher inspection, recharging, and compliance requirements.

How often do fire extinguishers need to be inspected in California?

California requires fire extinguishers to be inspected at multiple intervals, and each level of inspection has different requirements. The most frequent is the monthly visual inspection, which must be performed by a designated employee at your business. This is a quick check to confirm the extinguisher is in its designated location, the pressure gauge reads in the operable (green) zone, the pin and seal are intact, and the unit shows no obvious physical damage. While no certification is required to perform a monthly visual check, employees should be trained on what to look for.

The more thorough annual inspection must be performed by an OSFM-certified technician — this is required under the California Code of Regulations (CCR), Title 19. An annual inspection goes well beyond the visual check and results in a dated, signed inspection tag affixed to the unit. Failure to have annual inspections performed by a licensed technician means your extinguishers are technically out of compliance under California law, regardless of their physical condition.

Beyond annual inspections, NFPA 10 also requires a 6-year internal examination for stored-pressure dry chemical extinguishers. This involves completely discharging, disassembling, and inspecting the interior components before recharging. And at the intervals specified by NFPA 10 (typically every 5 or 12 years depending on type), hydrostatic testing of the cylinder is required to verify structural integrity. All of these requirements apply to California businesses, and our team can keep your facility on schedule for all of them.

What happens during a fire extinguisher inspection?

During an annual inspection, our OSFM-certified technician performs a methodical check of every critical component of each extinguisher. The pressure gauge is the first thing we check — it must read in the green (operable) zone. A gauge reading in the red zone (overcharged or undercharged) means the unit must be recharged before it can be returned to service. Next, we verify that the pull pin is fully inserted and properly positioned — a missing or partially seated pin means the extinguisher cannot be reliably activated in an emergency.

We also check the tamper seal to confirm the unit has not been discharged or tampered with since its last inspection. The discharge nozzle and hose (on units with hoses) are checked for obstructions, cracks, or deterioration. The cylinder body and label are examined for corrosion, dents, gouges, or any damage that could compromise the structural integrity of the unit or make the label unreadable. Illegible labels are a compliance issue — NFPA 10 requires that operating instructions be clearly visible at all times.

We also verify proper mounting and accessibility. Under NFPA 10, extinguishers must be mounted at the correct height, clearly visible, and accessible without obstruction. Extinguishers blocked by equipment or stored in closets fail this requirement. At the conclusion of the inspection, we affix a dated tag showing the month, year, and our technician's OSFM license number — as required by California law. We also provide documentation suitable for your compliance records.

How much does fire extinguisher recharging cost?

Recharging costs vary by extinguisher type, size, and agent. For the most common commercial unit — a standard ABC dry chemical stored-pressure extinguisher — recharging typically costs in the range of $15 to $40, depending on the size of the unit. Smaller units (2.5 lb) are at the lower end; larger units (10 lb and above) are at the higher end. CO2 extinguishers cost somewhat more to recharge due to the cost of the CO2 agent itself and the specialized filling equipment required.

Wet chemical and Class K extinguishers — used primarily in commercial kitchens — tend to cost more to recharge than ABC dry chemical units, reflecting the higher cost of the suppression agent. Specialty extinguishers such as clean agent (Halotron) types are also at the higher end of the recharging cost spectrum.

In nearly all cases, recharging is significantly more cost-effective than replacing the extinguisher outright. A new commercial ABC dry chemical unit typically costs $50 to $150 or more depending on size and rating. Recharging the same unit keeps it in service at a fraction of that cost. We always assess the condition of the unit before recommending recharging versus replacement — if an extinguisher is damaged, near the end of its service life, or has failed hydrostatic testing, replacement may be the right answer. What is hydrostatic testing for fire extinguishers?

Hydrostatic testing is a pressure test applied to a fire extinguisher cylinder to verify its structural integrity. Over years of use, the metal cylinder of a fire extinguisher is subjected to internal pressure, temperature changes, and handling stresses that can cause micro-fractures or weakening of the metal wall. These defects are not visible during a standard inspection. Hydrostatic testing pressurizes the empty cylinder to a level substantially above its rated working pressure — typically 150% of the service pressure — and measures whether the cylinder holds that pressure without expanding beyond acceptable limits, leaking, or failing.

NFPA 10 specifies the required hydrostatic testing intervals for each type of extinguisher. CO2 and water-based extinguishers require hydrostatic testing every 5 years. Stored-pressure dry chemical extinguishers require testing every 12 years. Halons and certain other specialty types are also tested on 12-year cycles. These intervals are not suggestions — they are compliance requirements in California, and extinguishers past their hydrostatic test due date are technically out of service.

Hydrostatic testing must be performed at a licensed facility with specialized equipment — it cannot be done in the field during a routine inspection. After testing, cylinders that pass are marked with the test date and returned to service. Cylinders that fail are condemned and must be replaced. We perform hydrostatic testing on all common extinguisher types and provide full documentation of results. If you're not sure when your extinguishers were last hydro-tested, check the stamped date on the cylinder or call us and we'll help you figure it out.

Do I need OSFM-certified technicians for fire extinguisher service in California?

Yes — California law requires that annual fire extinguisher inspections, recharging, and hydrostatic testing be performed by technicians licensed by the Office of the State Fire Marshal (OSFM). This requirement is established in the California Code of Regulations, Title 19. The OSFM licensing process involves training, examination, and ongoing compliance requirements designed to ensure that anyone performing these services meets a minimum standard of competency.

The practical implication for your business is straightforward: if your fire extinguishers are serviced by an unlicensed technician — or by someone claiming to be licensed who is not — the service tags on your equipment are legally invalid. This matters when the fire marshal inspects your facility, when a health department inspector conducts a routine visit, or when your insurance carrier reviews your compliance records. An invalid inspection tag is a compliance failure regardless of the physical condition of the extinguisher.

All Safe Fire Extinguisher Co. technicians are fully licensed by the California OSFM. Every inspection tag we affix includes the technician's OSFM license number as required by California law. When you work with us, you can be confident that your extinguishers are in compliance — not just maintained, but legally documented as compliant. If you have any questions about our certifications, we're happy to provide our license information upon request.

How many fire extinguishers does my restaurant need?

The number and type of fire extinguishers required in a restaurant is determined by a combination of NFPA 10, NFPA 96 (for commercial cooking operations), and your local fire code. At a minimum, any restaurant or commercial kitchen must have at least one Class K extinguisher located within 30 feet of the commercial cooking equipment. Class K extinguishers are specifically designed for fires involving cooking oils and greases — the type of fire most likely to occur in a kitchen environment — and they are the only type that meets NFPA 96 requirements for cooking equipment protection.

Beyond the Class K unit, your restaurant will also need ABC dry chemical extinguishers throughout the dining room, storage areas, and other non-cooking portions of the facility. Under NFPA 10, extinguishers must be placed so that the maximum travel distance from any point in the space to the nearest extinguisher does not exceed 75 feet for Class A hazards (ordinary combustibles like furniture and décor) or 50 feet for Class B hazards (flammable liquids). In practice, most restaurants will need at least two to four extinguishers total, depending on square footage and layout.

Health department inspectors in California routinely check for the presence and compliance of fire extinguishers during restaurant inspections. A missing Class K unit, an overdue inspection tag, or an extinguisher that is inaccessible or improperly mounted can result in a citation. What type of fire extinguisher do I need for my business?

Fire extinguishers are classified by the types of fires they are designed to fight. Understanding these classes is essential to selecting the right equipment for your business. Class A extinguishers fight ordinary combustible fires involving materials like wood, paper, fabric, and plastics — the type of fire most common in offices, retail spaces, and storage areas. Class B extinguishers are rated for flammable liquid fires involving gasoline, oil, solvents, and similar substances. Class C ratings indicate the extinguisher uses a non-conductive agent suitable for use on energized electrical equipment. Class D extinguishers are a specialty type for combustible metal fires and are typically only needed in industrial environments. Class K extinguishers are designed specifically for commercial kitchen fires involving cooking oils and greases.

The most common extinguisher type for general commercial use is the ABC dry chemical (multipurpose) extinguisher, which covers Class A, B, and C fires. These are the workhorses of fire protection and are appropriate for most office, retail, warehouse, and light manufacturing environments. However, ABC dry chemical is not appropriate as the sole extinguisher in a commercial kitchen — grease fires require a Class K unit that is specifically formulated for cooking oil fires.

Certain environments call for specialty extinguishers. Server rooms and data centers benefit from CO2 or clean agent extinguishers that suppress fires without leaving the residue that dry chemical agents do — residue that can destroy sensitive electronics. Facilities with specific chemical or metal hazards need type-appropriate units. When you work with All Safe, we assess your specific business operations, layout, and risk profile to recommend the correct types, sizes, and ratings for your extinguishers. There's no one-size-fits-all answer, and we take the time to get it right.

How long does a fire extinguisher last?

A well-maintained fire extinguisher can remain in service for 10 to 12 years, and in some cases longer, depending on the type of extinguisher and the conditions it has been exposed to. The key phrase is "well-maintained" — an extinguisher that has received regular annual inspections, been recharged when needed, and passed its required hydrostatic testing on schedule is far more likely to reach its full service life than one that has been neglected. Conversely, an extinguisher that was discharged and not recharged, stored in a harsh environment, or physically damaged may need to be replaced well before the 10-year mark.

California and NFPA 10 do not specify a mandatory replacement age for fire extinguishers the way some other jurisdictions do. Instead, an extinguisher remains in service as long as it continues to pass all required inspections and testing — including its periodic hydrostatic test. If an extinguisher fails the hydrostatic test, is damaged beyond repair, or has visible corrosion that compromises structural integrity, it must be condemned and replaced regardless of age. Most manufacturers, however, recommend replacement after 12 years as a general guideline.

The practical takeaway: don't assume an extinguisher is still good just because it's hanging on the wall. And don't assume it needs to be replaced just because it's old. The inspection and testing schedule exists precisely to make that determination accurately.
